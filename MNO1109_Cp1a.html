General Notes for Linesplan

A ship hull is made of curvy geometry. In order to represent the ship hull in drawing, naval architects use the Linesplan. See Figure above.

From Figure above, you can see that there are several planes that cut accross the ship:

1. The longitudinal plane (yellow plane)
2. The transverse plane (green plane)
3. The waterplane (red plane)

All these planes will trace the shape of the ships at three different directions, i.e. the x-direction (logitudinal plane), y-direction (transverse plane) and the z-direciton (waterplane).

The tracing of the shapes of the ships is called the projection of the intersection of the hull with its planes.

For each direction, there are several planes spaced at equal interval along the ships to capture the shapes of the ship.
1. All the projections in the longitudinal planes are plotted on the side view known as sheer plane.
2. All the projections in the transverse planes are plotted on the front view known as body plan.
3. All the projections in the waterplanes are plotted on the top view known as half-breadth plan.

These set of planes are mutually perpendicular or orthogonal in nature.

Description of Linesplan

The projection of ships can be made to three different planes, and resulted in three different views (projections), called the Body Plan, Half-Breadth Plan and Sheer (Profile) Plan.

For each projection, the ship geometry is represented by three lines, i.e.
1. Station
2. Buttock line
3. Waterline

Stations are equal interval spacing along the length of the ship, that starts from the After Perpendicular (AP) to the Forward Perpendicular (FP) .

Buttock line represents curve lines that are formed from intersection between the hull form and the longitudinal plane.

Waterlines are curve lines formed from intersection between ship hull and waterplane.

It is important to be able to visualise the difference between waterplane, transverse plane and longitudinal plane.

Various Coefficient

Coefficients of hull form allow the performance of the vessel to be evaluated and dictate the type of ship.

There are several types of hull coefficients, such as block coefficient, water-plane coefficient, midship area coefficient, prismatic coefficient

×
×

Midship Area Coefficient

The Midship Area Coefficient is defined as,

C_M=\frac{A_{midship}}{B \times T}

where
CM is the midship area coefficient
Amidship is the midship area of the ship
B is the breadth of the ship
T is the draft of the ship

×
×

Water-Plane Area Coefficient

The Water-plane Area Coefficient is defined as,

C_{WP}=\frac{A_{waterplane}}{L \times B}

where
CWP is the water-plane area coefficient
Awaterplane is the water-plane area of the ship
B is the breadth of the ship
L is the length of the ship

Block Coefficient

The Block Coefficient is defined as,

C_{B}=\frac{V}{L \times B \times T}

where
CB is the block Coefficient
V is the displacement volume
B is the breadth of the ship
L is the length of the ship
T is the draft of the ship

Prismatic Coefficient
Another way of combining the Block Coefficient CB and Midship-area Coefficient CM is by presenting them as Prismatic Coefficient. This is given by

\frac{C_B}{C_M}=\frac{V}{A_{midship} \times L}

where
V is the displacement volume
Amidship is the midship area
L is the length of the ship

Equilibrium

Principle of Archimedes states that the upward buoyant force that is exerted on a body immersed in a fluid, whether fully or partially submerged, is equal to the weight of the fluid that the body displaces and acts in the upward direction at the center of mass of the displaced fluid.

The buoyancy force is measured as the weight of the displaced volume.

The ship will sink if the weight is greater than the buoyancy and will rise when the buoyancy is greater than the weight.

When the weight is equal to the buoyancy, the ship is said to be in equilibrium position.

Tonnage
Tonnage is used to measure the size and load carrying capacity of the ship.

It is a volume measurement and not a weight measurement.

One standard ton = 100 cubic feet.

There are two different tonnages, i.e.
1. gross tonnage - represent overall size of a ship and use to measure the difficulty of handling and berthing,
2. Net tonnage - represent its carrying capacity and used to measure the earning ability of the ship

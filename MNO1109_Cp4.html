Moment of Statical Stability
When an external force such as wind is applied to the side of the ship, the ship will heel. As a result, the CoG moves from B to B1 , which is parallel to GG1.

Remember that the force of buoyancy acts vertically upwards through B1 whereas the weight of the ship acts vertically downwards through the CoG.

If you draw a perpendicular distance between G to the vertical line through B1, you will obtain the intersection point Z.

The distance GZ is call the rightling lever, and it represents the perpendicular distance between the lines of actions of forces (Weight and Buoyancy).

By taking the moments about the CoG, the moment of statical stability is equal to the product of the righting lever and the displacement, i.e

Moment of Statical Stability = W \times GZ

Stable Equilibrium

When a ship is in stable equilibrium, she tends to return to the initial position when if it inclined.

The equilibrium (stability) of the ship can be defined by its GM value.

For Stable equilibrium, the GM value has to be greater than 0, i.e. GM>0.



×
×

Unstable Equilibrium

For GM lesser than 0, the ship is said to be in unstable equilibrium.

Under such condition, the ship will sink.

Neutral Equilibrium

If GM = 0, the ship is in neutral equilibrium. In neutral equilibrium, KG=KM.

If the ship is inclied to a small angle, the ship will remain at that angle of heel until acted by an external force.
